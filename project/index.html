EvOCRO – Evolutionary Origins of Cognition in Crocodiles

EvOCRO investigates how learning and cognitive flexibility develop across the lifespan of crocodiles and how individual differences shape these abilities, using Morelet’s crocodile as a model system.

Publications Team

Why crocodiles?

  • Crocodiles belong to Archosauria and diverged from birds over 300 million years ago, offering a key branch for comparative cognition.
  • They show complex behaviour but remain understudied because of logistical and safety challenges in the field.
  • Understanding their cognition can inform conservation, animal welfare and the design of better enrichment and release programmes.

Research objectives

  • RO1 – Ontogeny of learning: how cognitive abilities change from hatchlings to juveniles of different ages.
  • RO2 – Individual differences: how traits such as boldness and exploration influence learning performance.
  • RO3 – Cognitive flexibility: how crocodiles adapt to new information in reversal learning tasks.

Approach

EvOCRO combines behavioural ecology, comparative psychology and evolutionary biology in a partnership between Anglia Ruskin University (UK) and CEDESU (Mexico). Controlled farm environments provide access to diverse Crocodylus moreletii populations and allow systematic, repeatable experiments under ecologically relevant conditions.

Why crocodiles?

Crocodiles are long-lived predators with sophisticated sensory systems and flexible behaviour, yet their cognition has been explored far less than that of birds or mammals. Studying cognition in crocodilians helps to bridge an important evolutionary gap within Archosauria and to challenge the bias that only “classically complex” species are suitable models for cognitive research.

At the same time, personality traits such as boldness, shyness or exploration can affect how individuals learn, cope with environmental change and survive in the wild. Linking cognition to these traits is highly relevant for conservation programmes, rehabilitation and reintroduction, where understanding which animals are more likely to thrive can improve management decisions.

Research objectives

EvOCRO focuses on Morelet’s crocodile (Crocodylus moreletii) through a strategic partnership with CEDESU in Campeche, Mexico. The project addresses three main research objectives:

  • RO1 – Ontogeny of learning (WP1): quantify how learning abilities develop from hatchlings to juveniles (6 months, 1 year, 2 years), including differences between sexes.
  • RO2 – Individual differences in learning (WP2): test how stable behavioural traits along the exploration–shyness spectrum relate to learning performance.
  • RO3 – Cognitive flexibility (WP3): assess how crocodiles update their responses when reward contingencies change, using reversal learning tasks to measure flexibility.

Approach and work packages

EvOCRO is organised into interconnected work packages that combine developmental, individual and comparative perspectives with strong training, management and outreach components.

WP1 – Ontogeny of learning

WP1 examines how learning develops from hatching to the juvenile stage. Crocodiles are trained with a simple visual cue using positive reinforcement, followed by a two-choice discrimination task. Once a learning criterion is reached, reward contingencies are reversed to test how performance changes with age and sex.

WP2 – Individual differences

WP2 uses the same cohort of animals to measure personality traits such as exploration and response to novelty or potential threats. These traits are then linked to learning outcomes from WP1 to understand how intrinsic behavioural variation shapes cognitive performance.

WP3 – Cognitive flexibility

WP3 builds on the familiarisation and learning tasks developed in WP1 to investigate cognitive flexibility. By systematically reversing stimulus–reward associations, the project assesses how quickly and accurately individuals adapt to new rules, and how this relates to age, personality and past performance.

WP4 & WP5 – Training, management and communication

WP4 covers project management and the researcher’s career development, including training in advanced statistical methods, open science practices and interdisciplinary collaboration. WP5 focuses on communication, dissemination and public engagement, through scientific outputs, workshops, outreach activities and digital content.

Impact

Scientifically, EvOCRO will expand knowledge of reptile cognition, provide longitudinal data on learning and flexibility, and contribute to comparative work across taxa beyond the usual model species.

From an applied perspective, the findings can inform conservation strategies, enrichment design and welfare practices for crocodiles in farms, zoos and protected areas. The project is also aligned with several UN Sustainable Development Goals by linking research, conservation and public engagement.
